25// A three-player matching pennies, described in (J. S. Jordan. Three problems
26// in learning mixed-strategy Nash equilibria. Games and Economic Behavior,
27// 5:368–386, 1993. Also described in section 1.3 of these notes:
28// http://web.stanford.edu/~rjohari/teaching/notes/336_lecture7_2007.pdf
29//
30// From the notes: "Each player has two actions, H or T. Player 1 wants to match
31// the action of player 2; player 2 wants to match the action of player 3; and
32// player 3 wants to match the opposite of the action of player 1. Each player
33// receives a payoff of 1 if they match as desired, and −1 otherwise. It is
34// straightforward to check that this game has a unique Nash equilibrium, where
35// all players uniformly randomize. Jordan shows that this Nash equilibrium is
36// locally unstable in a strong sense: for any epsilon > 0, and for almost all
37// initial empirical distributions that are within (Euclidean) distance epsilon
38// of the unique Nash equilibrium, discrete-time fictitious play does not
39// converge to the NE; instead, it enters a limit cycle asymptotically as t ->
40// infinity".
